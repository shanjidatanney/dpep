About Me

Early Childhood Educator & Student

My name is Shanjida Shahin Tanney, and I am currently studying a Bachelor of Early Childhood Education at the IKON Institute of Australia. Alongside my studies, I am working as a Diploma-qualified educator at Jenny's ELC, The Arch, where I also had the valuable opportunity to complete my professional placement.

My Passion & Philosophy

I am deeply passionate about creating inclusive, nurturing, and engaging learning environments where all children feel respected, supported, and valued. I believe that every child is unique, with their own strengths, abilities, and ways of learning, and I strive to foster an environment that celebrates diversity and encourages children to explore their world with curiosity and confidence.

Current Role & Experience

In my current role, I focus on building strong, respectful relationships with children and families while supporting each child's holistic development. I enjoy designing creative, play-based experiences that encourage problem-solving, self-expression, and social interaction, while also promoting a sense of belonging in the classroom.

What Drives Me

For me, the most rewarding part of being an educator is witnessing children grow in confidence, develop new skills, and feel proud of their achievements. I value collaboration with colleagues and actively reflect on my practice to ensure I am continuously improving and providing the best learning opportunities for children.

Future Aspirations

As I continue my Bachelor studies, I am eager to expand my knowledge of pedagogy, strengthen my practical teaching skills, and gain more experience across diverse educational settings. My long-term aspiration is to become a professional and reflective early childhood teacher who empowers children to reach their full potential and fosters a lifelong love of learning.

Overview of Jenny's Early Learning Centre - The Arch

During my placement at Jenny's Early Learning Centre -- The Arch, I had the opportunity to explore the thoughtfully designed layout and learning environment of the service. From the moment you walk in, the reception area warmly welcomes families and visitors. This space leads directly to a spacious planning room, where educators prepare programs, reflect on their teaching practices, and design engaging learning experiences.

Centre Layout & Facilities

The centre is divided into six classrooms, each tailored to specific age groups and thoughtfully designed to support children's development at each stage of their learning journey:

Room 1: Lawson Room

Infants (0-1 years)

A peaceful, sensory-rich space for infants, featuring soft textures, quiet rest areas, and exploration zones that stimulate early sensory and tactile development.

Room 2: Eureka Room

Toddlers (1-2 years)

A lively and interactive environment filled with sensory play materials that promote motor skills, communication, and early social interactions in a bright, engaging setting.

Room 3: Lalor Room

Toddlers (2-3 years)

A creative play area designed for art, building, and pretend play, encouraging toddlers to explore their independence and engage meaningfully with their peers.

Room 4: Remembrance Room

Junior Kinder (3-4 years)

A space that introduces structured learning through educational games, early literacy activities, and group interactions, helping children develop teamwork and problem-solving skills.

Room 5: Begonia Room

Kinder (4-5 years)

A project-based learning space where children engage in hands-on experiences such as building, art, and basic science experiments, promoting critical thinking and curiosity.

Room 6: Memorial Room

Senior Kinder (5-6 years)

A versatile area used for physical activities, group projects, and collaborative events. This space encourages physical development, cooperative play, and gross motor skill building.

Facilities & Safety Features

Across the centre, classrooms are equipped with age-appropriate learning resources, and each age group has dedicated spaces for toileting, safe rest, and food preparation. For every two classrooms, there is one shared toilet area, each fitted with three age-appropriate toilet seats. The nappy changing stations are highly secure and designed with child safety as the top priority.

Outdoor Play Spaces

The outdoor play spaces are thoughtfully divided into two areas:

Backyard

Includes a sandpit, a calm grassy sitting area, climbing beams, and a swing zone.

Front Yard

Designed for younger children aged 0-2 years, featuring safe play equipment such as a sandpit, foam blocks, low slides, and small climbing beams. Several gardens with bark, stones, and natural elements are also available, where educators often facilitate nature exploration activities to support children's connection to the environment.

Overall, Jenny's ELC -- The Arch is a highly child-friendly centre, designed to promote exploration, independence, social interaction, and holistic development in a safe and nurturing environment.

Philosophy of Jenny's Early Learning Centre - The Arch

Child-Led, Play-Based Learning

At Jenny's Early Learning Centre -- The Arch, the educational approach is firmly rooted in child-led, play-based learning, with a strong emphasis on creating an environment that nurtures curiosity, creativity, and independence (Edwards, Gandini, & Forman, 2012).

Reggio Emilia Philosophy

The centre follows the Reggio Emilia philosophy, which views children as active participants in their own learning journey. In this approach, the environment is considered a "third teacher," thoughtfully designed to stimulate exploration, imagination, and inquiry (Rinaldi, 2006).

Core Values & Practices

Relationship Building

Educators at the centre prioritise building strong, respectful, and meaningful relationships with children and their families, fostering a sense of belonging, emotional safety, and trust (Dahlberg, Moss, & Pence, 2013).

Celebrating Diversity

The centre actively celebrates diversity and is committed to implementing inclusive practices that reflect and respect each child's unique identity, culture, and background (Australian Children's Education & Care Quality Authority [ACECQA], 2020).

Holistic Development

Children are encouraged to explore, express themselves, and engage with their surroundings in ways that support their holistic development. Through play, creativity, and guided experiences, children develop essential cognitive, emotional, social, and physical skills, laying a strong foundation for lifelong learning (Siraj-Blatchford et al., 2002).

Individualized Learning

The approach ensures that learning is meaningful, engaging, and tailored to each child's individual needs, interests, and abilities, allowing them to grow with confidence and a strong sense of self (Department of Education, Skills and Employment [DESE], 2022).

Dimensions of Professionalism

Adhering to Professionalism in Early Childhood Education

Topic Focus

My placement emphasized the importance of professionalism, including maintaining ethical standards, effective communication, and a commitment to continuous improvement.

Practical Example

Throughout my placement, I made sure to arrive on time, prepared for each activity, and communicate effectively with both colleagues and children. For instance, on Day 5, I led a group reading session with clarity and patience, ensuring that all children could follow along and participate. I also ensured children followed proper hygiene practices, such as washing their hands thoroughly after messy activities, including a snow sensory activity.

The centre itself demonstrates professionalism through careful attention to staffing ratios, ensuring quality supervision and engagement. In the infant and toddler rooms, there is a ratio of four children per educator, while in the kindergarten rooms, there is one educator for every twelve children, supporting safe and individualized care.

Professional reflection is also embedded into daily practice: each classroom has daily and weekly reflection books, where educators can document their observations, the children's engagement, and any notable outcomes from activities. Additionally, each educator is responsible for observing and documenting four children, completing jottings and observations to guide further planning. The centre also organizes monthly meetings for team reflection, discussion, and review of educational practices to ensure continuous improvement.

Theoretical Foundation

The Australian Professional Standards for Teachers (APST) emphasize the importance of professionalism in all aspects of teaching, including communication, ethical behavior, and continuous reflection and improvement (AITSL, 2011). Similarly, NQS Standard 4 highlights the need for educators to maintain professionalism in all interactions and practices to support quality education and care. These practices, both personal and organizational, ensure that educators provide safe, reflective, and high-quality learning experiences for every child.

Play-Based Learning and Educational Environment

Fostering Learning Through Play and Creating Engaging Environments

Topic Focus

Play-based learning was a central aspect of my practice, offering children opportunities to explore, experiment, and develop a wide range of cognitive, social, emotional, and physical skills within a safe and stimulating environment.

Practical Example

On Day 6, I led an outdoor science-inspired activity where children created "snow" using salt and vinegar. This sensory-rich experience supported their natural curiosity and introduced them to early scientific concepts such as cause-and-effect and chemical reactions (Edwards, 2017). It also engaged their fine motor skills as they poured, mixed, and observed changes.

Indoors, I encouraged children to use playdough to make animals, which promoted creativity, symbolic representation, and problem-solving (Wood, 2014). The children compared their creations, exchanged ideas, and extended each other's play, which reflects the socially interactive nature of learning. I scaffolded their experiences by asking open-ended questions like, "What do you think will happen next?" or "How can you make your animal stand?" These prompts stimulated critical thinking, prediction-making, and extended language use (Fleer, 2010).

Why it Matters

Play provided children with a safe, meaningful, and engaging avenue for exploration, where learning occurred naturally through discovery and social participation (Bodrova & Leong, 2015). Through play, children not only experimented with ideas but also built confidence and resilience when their strategies did not initially succeed. This aligns with the Early Years Learning Framework (EYLF), which identifies play as a foundation for fostering agency, problem-solving, and collaboration (Department of Education, 2018).

Theoretical Foundation

Vygotsky (1978) highlights that play is the "leading activity" in early childhood, allowing children to engage in higher-order thinking through social interaction and scaffolding within their Zone of Proximal Development. Piaget's (1962) theory also supports this practice, describing play as a way for children to construct knowledge and move through stages of cognitive development. The EYLF (Department of Education, 2018) further emphasizes that play-based pedagogies nurture holistic development, including identity, belonging, communication, and wellbeing.

Respectful Engagement and Inclusive Practices

Building Respectful Relationships and Promoting Inclusivity

Topic Focus

Respectful engagement and inclusivity were at the core of my interactions with children, forming the basis for positive learning experiences and social-emotional wellbeing.

Practical Example

During a reading session on Day 5, I encouraged a child named Raiden when he confidently identified objects in the story such as "apple" and "crab." By acknowledging his enthusiasm and providing specific positive feedback, I reinforced his confidence, made him feel included, and encouraged his peers to participate as well.

I also adapted my communication strategies to meet children's different needs, using gestures, visual cues, and simplified language for younger children who were still developing verbal skills. For example, when engaging with a toddler who pointed at a picture, I modeled the word aloud and combined it with a gesture, ensuring inclusivity by making learning accessible for diverse learners.

Why it Matters

Inclusivity ensures that every child, regardless of their learning stage, cultural background, or developmental abilities, feels valued and respected. Research highlights that when children experience respect and recognition, their sense of identity and belonging is strengthened, which is essential for both confidence and engagement in learning (Arthur et al., 2018).

Theoretical Foundation

EYLF Outcome 1 emphasizes the importance of supporting children's identity and sense of belonging through respectful and culturally responsive interactions (Department of Education, 2018). Similarly, the National Quality Standard (NQS) Standard 5 highlights the value of responsive and respectful relationships as a foundation for children's social development and wellbeing (ACECQA, 2020).

Cultural Respect and Playful Opportunities

Incorporating Cultural Respect and Playful Learning Opportunities

Topic Focus

Respect for cultural diversity was a central focus of my teaching approach, ensuring that children developed an understanding and appreciation of different cultures, particularly during NAIDOC Week, which provided rich opportunities for learning about Australia's First Nations peoples.

Practical Example

On Day 2, I actively supported a smoking ceremony and facilitated a cultural music session with the children. They not only observed the rituals but also engaged by asking thoughtful questions and expressing curiosity about Indigenous practices.

I extended this learning by linking cultural experiences with playful activities, such as dancing to traditional music, creating artworks inspired by Indigenous symbols, and storytelling sessions that explored cultural narratives. These experiences encouraged children to express themselves creatively while deepening their understanding of cultural traditions.

Why it Matters

Embedding cultural respect into everyday learning experiences fosters children's cultural competence, awareness, and empathy, which are essential for forming inclusive attitudes from a young age (Arthur et al., 2018). Exposure to diverse cultural practices helps children develop respect for others' traditions and promotes social cohesion.

Theoretical Foundation

Cultural competence is a key component of the EYLF, which emphasizes that educators should respect and promote children's cultural identity and diversity in all learning contexts (Department of Education, 2018). Vygotsky's sociocultural theory (1978) further supports this practice by recognizing that children learn through social interaction and culturally mediated experiences.

Child Development and Assessment

Supporting Holistic Child Development Through Observation and Assessment

Topic Focus

Observation and assessment were essential components of my practice, enabling me to understand children's developmental progress, identify their strengths and needs, and tailor learning experiences to support holistic growth.

Practical Example

On Day 9, I introduced an alphabet tracing activity (A, B, C, D) to observe children's early literacy skills and fine motor development. While some children confidently held pencils and formed letters with precision, others required guidance and encouragement, which I carefully documented for future scaffolding.

I also observed children's social interactions, such as taking turns, sharing materials, and offering peer support, highlighting their cognitive, social, and emotional development. These observations were recorded in reflection books, providing a structured way to track progress, inform curriculum planning, and ensure targeted support for individual learners.

Why it Matters

Ongoing assessment enables educators to understand children's individual learning trajectories, identify areas where support is needed, and celebrate achievements, fostering confidence and motivation (Arthur et al., 2018). Holistic assessment also ensures that cognitive, social-emotional, and physical development are addressed in tandem.

Theoretical Foundation

Piaget's cognitive development theory (1952) highlights the importance of hands-on, experiential learning, where children actively construct knowledge through interaction with materials and their peers. The EYLF (Department of Education, 2018) reinforces this by promoting Outcome 5, which emphasizes that children should develop early literacy and numeracy skills through intentional and playful learning experiences.

Responsive Engagement with Children

Engaging Respectfully and Responsively with Children

Topic Focus

Respectful and responsive engagement with children fostered trust, a sense of belonging, and positive social-emotional development, which are essential for creating supportive and meaningful learning experiences.

Practical Example

On Day 2, I provided children with the choice of playing indoors or outdoors, intentionally respecting their autonomy and individual preferences. Some children chose to explore nature outdoors, engaging with the sandpit, climbing equipment, and small garden areas, while others remained inside for creative activities.

During the outdoor exploration, I scaffolded learning by asking open-ended questions like, "What can you build with these sticks?" and "How do you think water moves in the sand?" which promoted problem-solving, curiosity, and collaborative thinking.

Why it Matters

Encouraging autonomy and responsive engagement fosters children's confidence, self-regulation, decision-making, and responsibility (Arthur et al., 2018). It also cultivates trust between children and educators, contributing to a positive learning environment where children feel safe to explore and express themselves.

Theoretical Foundation

Erikson's psychosocial theory (1963) emphasizes autonomy as a critical developmental milestone in early childhood, supporting the child's emerging independence, self-esteem, and social competence. This aligns with EYLF Outcome 1, which focuses on children developing a strong sense of identity and belonging through respectful relationships (Department of Education, 2018).

National Curriculum Frameworks and Quality Standards

Aligning Practices with National Curriculum and Quality Standards

Topic Focus

My practice consistently aligned with national curriculum frameworks and quality standards to ensure that all learning experiences were meaningful, developmentally appropriate, and supported holistic child outcomes.

Practical Example

On Day 7, I participated in Pyjama Week, a themed activity where children drew and discussed their clothing. This activity not only encouraged self-expression but also supported emotional awareness, storytelling, and reflective thinking. Children shared stories about their favourite pyjamas, discussed colours and patterns, and related their clothing choices to personal experiences.

I documented the activity in detail, noting each child's engagement, language use, creativity, and social interactions. By reflecting on these observations, I ensured that the activity met the learning objectives outlined in the Early Years Learning Framework (EYLF) and was aligned with the National Quality Standard (NQS) guidelines.

Why it Matters

Aligning practice with national frameworks ensures that all educational activities are purposeful, evidence-based, and structured to support long-term developmental goals (Arthur et al., 2018). It allows educators to maintain consistency in teaching, plan experiences that are inclusive and culturally responsive, and measure outcomes effectively.

Theoretical Foundation

The EYLF emphasizes play-based and intentional learning experiences, guiding educators to support children's holistic development across social, emotional, cognitive, and physical domains (Department of Education, 2018). The NQS provides a framework to ensure high-quality education and care, particularly focusing on standards related to educational program and practice.

Holistic Child Development and Learning Dispositions

Supporting Holistic Development and Dispositions for Learning

Topic Focus

Supporting children's holistic development, encompassing emotional, social, cognitive, and physical wellbeing, was a central focus of my placement. Emphasizing dispositions for learning such as resilience, cooperation, curiosity, and persistence helped foster confident, engaged, and capable learners.

Practical Example

On Day 6, I assisted children with cutting paper using scissors during a creative arts activity. This task supported the development of fine motor skills, hand-eye coordination, and concentration, which are essential for later literacy and self-care abilities.

Additionally, the activity encouraged social-emotional growth: children shared scissors, took turns, and supported peers who struggled. I observed children regulating their emotions when mistakes occurred, showing persistence rather than frustration, and celebrating each other's successes.

I extended the experience by asking reflective questions, such as, "How did you feel when you helped your friend?" or "What strategies helped you cut the paper carefully?" These prompts encouraged children to develop metacognitive awareness, empathy, and cooperative problem-solving skills.

I also documented these observations in daily reflection books, linking them to individual learning goals and broader developmental outcomes, which informed future planning for both targeted interventions and group activities.

Why it Matters

Activities that integrate physical, cognitive, and social-emotional learning provide children with balanced, holistic development. Developing dispositions for learning such as resilience, persistence, curiosity, and collaboration lays the foundation for lifelong learning, problem-solving, and positive relationships (Arthur et al., 2018; Siraj-Blatchford et al., 2002).

Theoretical Foundation

Bronfenbrenner's ecological systems theory (1979) emphasizes the interconnected nature of development across environments, highlighting the importance of interactions between children, educators, peers, family, and the broader community. EYLF Outcome 3 reinforces this perspective by stressing children's wellbeing and holistic development, integrating emotional, social, physical, and cognitive growth (Department of Education, 2018).

Safety, Wellbeing, and Curriculum Standards

Ensuring Safety and Wellbeing While Adhering to Curriculum Standards

Topic Focus

Maintaining the safety and wellbeing of children was a top priority in all aspects of my placement. I consistently integrated health, safety, and hygiene practices into daily routines and learning experiences to create a secure and supportive environment where children could explore, learn, and develop with confidence.

Practical Example

On Day 2, I supervised a clay activity during NAIDOC Week, ensuring children followed hygiene routines, including washing their hands before and after the activity. While engaging in the task, children explored textures, shapes, and patterns, which supported fine motor development and creativity. I also facilitated a drawing activity with the Aboriginal flag, linking cultural awareness with wellbeing by encouraging discussion about identity, respect, and community.

During these activities, I embedded safety routines into creative tasks: for instance, I reminded children to handle tools carefully, monitored their movement to prevent collisions, and provided gentle guidance when sharing materials. These practices helped children learn responsibility, self-regulation, and awareness of others' safety, while participating meaningfully in culturally rich and inclusive experiences.

Why it Matters

Safety and wellbeing form the foundation for learning and development; children who feel secure are more likely to engage, take risks in learning, and develop positive social-emotional skills (Siraj-Blatchford et al., 2002). Embedding these practices within the curriculum ensures holistic, balanced growth, supporting physical health, emotional regulation, and cultural competence.

Theoretical Foundation

Maslow's hierarchy of needs (1943) identifies safety and belonging as foundational for higher-order learning and personal growth. The EYLF also emphasizes creating safe, supportive, and healthy environments that protect children and enable active participation in meaningful learning experiences (Department of Education, 2018).

Advocacy for Children's Wellbeing

Advocating for Children's Safety and Emotional Needs

Topic Focus

Advocating for children's wellbeing was a fundamental professional responsibility during my placement, ensuring that every child's physical, emotional, and social needs were prioritised. Effective advocacy required observing, understanding, and responding to children's behaviours, emotions, and needs while also communicating with colleagues and families to provide holistic support.

Practical Example

Throughout my placement, I observed children experiencing a range of emotions, from frustration to sadness or excitement. On several occasions, when children became upsetâ€”such as during transitions between activities or after disagreements with peersâ€”I comforted them by kneeling to their level, speaking gently, and validating their feelings. For example, when a child felt overwhelmed during a painting activity, I acknowledged their emotions and suggested a calming strategy, such as taking deep breaths before returning to their artwork.

In addition to direct support, I recorded these observations in reflection books and shared them with colleagues to ensure ongoing and consistent care. This included noting triggers, preferred strategies for calming, and social interactions, allowing educators to plan targeted interventions and promote children's emotional resilience. I also communicated with families when necessary, providing insights into their child's wellbeing and collaborating on strategies to support them both at home and in the centre.

Why it Matters

Advocacy is essential in fostering children's safety, emotional security, and confidence. By actively supporting their wellbeing, educators create a foundation for learning where children feel safe, respected, and empowered to engage with their environment. Promoting emotional literacy and coping strategies also encourages long-term social competence, empathy, and resilience (Denham & Burton, 2003; Hughes & Coplan, 2018).

Theoretical Foundation

The Early Years Learning Framework (EYLF) Outcome 3 highlights the importance of supporting children's emotional wellbeing, social development, and sense of connectedness (Department of Education, 2018). NQS Standard 2 emphasizes health, safety, and wellbeing as core priorities, ensuring that services actively protect and nurture every child (ACECQA, 2020).

Creativity Across the Service and in Curriculum Provision

Encouraging Creativity Through Curriculum and Play

Topic Focus

Creativity was embedded throughout the curriculum at Jenny's ELC -- The Arch, not only to promote imaginative thinking but also to encourage resourcefulness and sustainability. The centre actively uses everyday and recycled materials to design engaging, environmentally conscious activities that support holistic child development.

Practical Example

Children regularly engaged in activities that incorporated recycled or unused materials. For instance, recycled bottles were used for simple science experiments, such as exploring floating and sinking or creating "snow" mixtures with salt and vinegar. During paper cutting activities, instead of discarding scraps, the children repurposed the cut paper for "pasting and collage" projects, which encouraged creativity while teaching them about reusing resources.

Unused cardboard was transformed into building materials for construction play or imaginative play setups, allowing children to explore spatial thinking and problem-solving while learning about sustainability. These practices not only nurtured creativity but also promoted collaboration, experimentation, and critical thinking as children discovered new ways to use materials for play and learning.

Why it Matters

Using recycled and everyday materials in creative activities encourages children to think innovatively, make connections between learning and real-life practices, and develop an awareness of environmental responsibility. This approach supports holistic development, including fine motor skills, cognitive reasoning, and social collaboration, while fostering a sense of agency and care for the environment (Edwards, 2012; Robinson, 2011).

Theoretical Foundation

Gardner's (1983) Multiple Intelligences theory highlights that children learn in diverse ways, and creative expression supports multiple domains, including visual-spatial, linguistic, and interpersonal intelligences. The EYLF also values creative expression for identity formation, problem-solving, and exploration (Department of Education, 2018). By embedding sustainability into creative activities, the centre aligns play-based learning with real-world values, reinforcing curiosity, responsibility, and environmental awareness.
